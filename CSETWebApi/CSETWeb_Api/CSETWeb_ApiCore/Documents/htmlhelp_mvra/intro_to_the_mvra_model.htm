1. Intro to the MVRA Model

 
Purpose
The purpose of the Minimum Viable Resilience Assessment (MVRA) User Guide is to assist assessors in conducting MVRAs in a consistent manner.
 
This User Guide:
  • presents an overview of the assessment structure and content;
  • provides information on how to prepare for an assessment;
  • provides information on how to conduct the assessment; and
  • provides guidance for follow-on activities.
 
The User Guide is intended to be used with the following items:
  • CSET MVRA Assessment, a tool that facilitates the assessment and records assessment findings, and
  • Guidance, references, and source documents. This additional material provides detailed explanations of questions asked to determine if a capability is fully implemented, partially implemented, or not implemented.
 
Audience
The primary audience of the User Guide are MVRA Assessment Team Leads (ALs) and Technical Leads (TLs). In addition, the User Guide will help an Assessment Point of Contact (APOC) better understand the assessment process.
 
Goals and Value Proposition
The MVRA has four primary goals:
1.     Assess the ability of entities of interest to “resist, absorb, recover from, or adapt to an adverse occurrence,” including the capacity to “recognize threats and hazards and make adjustments that will improve future protection efforts.”
2.     Document entities’ resilience on a scale of progressive level of capability.
3.     Improve the repeatability and consistency of assessments and the associated data gathered, thus both enhancing visibility and understanding of the observed trends, and increasing the defensibility of the mitigation recommendations.
4.     Employ standardized, repeatable data-gathering processes and automation that produce machine-readable data to facilitate the analysis of assessment results at scale.
 
The MVRA accomplishes this by incorporating significant elements from a number of assessments and frameworks into a single assessment that is broadly applicable to Strategically Important Entities (SIE), High Value Assets (HVA), Federal Civilian Executive Branch (FCEB), State, local, tribal, and territorial (SLTT) governments, and critical infrastructure.
 
Methodology
An MVRA assesses resilience, and is organized according to the structure of the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF), CISA Cyber Essentials, and NIST 800-160 Vol 2. Rev 1 (NIST 800-160). An assessment employs questions with pre-defined answer sets to determine the elements that an organization is implementing. Questions are grouped by topic to facilitate a modular assessment approach and simplify schedule of subject matter experts (SMEs). Evidence is gathered primarily through examination of artifacts and information provided in interviews. Vulnerability scans and penetration tests also may be conducted to assess systems directly.
 
Modality
CISA facilitates an MVRA with the expectation that an assessment team includes an AL and a minimum of one TL. The CISA Cyber Security Evaluation Tool (CSET) is the assessment instrument for self-assessments and facilitated assessments.
 
Architecture
An MVRA’s structure is based on the CSF, NIST 800-160, and CISA Cyber Essentials:
1.     Functions – The core resilience elements as defined by 800-160: Anticipate, Withstand, Recover, and Adapt. These are the highest level in an MVRA assessment and serve as thematic groupings.
2.     Domains – Logical groupings of capabilities, domains help assessors and entities being assessed organize documents and staff to be interviewed. The MVRA assigns a resilience score to each domain based on the number of fully implemented capabilities within that domain.
3.     Capabilities – The fundamental building blocks of an MVRA, capabilities are actions, policies, procedures, practices, controls, personnel, and other elements of cybersecurity that contribute to resilience. Capabilities are organized in three tiers based on the difficulty and expense of implementation: basic, intermediate, and advanced. Capabilities are scored as fully implemented, partially implemented, or not implemented based on a series of questions that help assessors determine if a capability is operational. Questions may be answered by documents, interview responses, or demonstrations. Criteria for full and partial implementation of a given capability are defined in the MVRA Question Guidance.
 
Underlying Framework
An MVRA leverages the organizing structure of NIST 800-160 and the CSF. The assessment draws on insights from the HVA Assessment, Cyber Essentials, Cyber Resilience Review (CRR), the Resilience Management Model (RMM), and other CISA assessments, as well as risk insights (e.g., CISA Bad Practices) that are currently deployed or in development.
 
Assessment Instrument
The original assessment instrument consisted of a Microsoft Excel spreadsheet on which assessors enter the scoring. Simple macros helped an assessor calculate a final score, and provided heat maps to support presentations to stakeholders.
 
The MVRA has now been incorporated into CSET, which will be used as the primary assessment instrument by late 2023.
 
Approved Assessor Tools and Techniques
The assessment instrument is the only tool employed in an MVRA. Additional services such as vulnerability scans and penetration tests may be offered.
 
Assessment Reporting Capability and Format
 
Capability
The MVRA assessment in CSET includes an automated reporting capability that aggregates and displays findings from the assessment, and highlights areas of concern. Assessment team members review the findings, supplement or amplify the automated report, and generate recommendations as to how to reduce the risk profile and improve the resilience of the assessed system.
 
Format
CSET assessment data is easily exported to a standard Excel or .csetw format for ingestion by CISA data analytics teams. Human-readable reporting is available in PDF format.
